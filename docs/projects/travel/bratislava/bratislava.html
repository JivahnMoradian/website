SLOVAKIA

Bratislava

Bratislava is the capital city of Slovakia. Located to the far West of the country, it is a mere 60km from Vienna, and is also a short 3 hour bus ride from Budapest. Although Bratislava was small, easily visitable in a day, and lacks the glamor of Vienna, it is a very charming and quaint city which is pleasant to walk around. Its city center was car-free, presented a variety of charming statues and fountains, and offered a great view of the the impressive Bratislava Castle. The city also has an extensive bus and tram service, which were impressively well-run: all the buses that I took within the city came exactly when they were scheduled to arrive.

Czechoslovakia was part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire until World War I, after which it gained its independence in 1918. It was occupied by Nazi Germany during World War II, and was liberated by Soviet forces in 1945. It consequently became a single-party communist satellite state of the USSR during the Cold War. In 1955, Czechoslovakia joined the Warsaw Pact, which was a mutual defense treaty between the USSR and most communist-block Eastern European nations formed in response to the West's NATO. In 1968, the relatively liberal Alexander Dubcek rose to power within Czechoslovakia's communist party, and implemented a series of reforms. His actions angered the Soviet Union, which later that year invaded Czechoslovakia alongside Bulgaria, Poland, and Hungary, and ultimately deposed Dubcek. This prompted a rare split in the Warsaw Pact countries: Romania strongly criticized the invasion, and Albania actually left the Pact in protest.

Czechoslovakia remained under Soviet influence until 1989, when a series of relatively peaceful protests and demonstrations culminated in the existing communist government resigning. It was replaced by the Civic Forum led by Vaclav Havel, a man who had opposed the communist regime, and had spent several years imprisoned as a political dissident. This Velvet Revolution led to the country holding its first free elections in 1990, and gradually opening up to the West. In 1993, following a vote held by Czechoslovakia's parliament, the country dissolved into the two present-day nations of Slovakia and the Czech Republic. Given its lack of violence, this seperation is sometimes referred to as the "Velvet Divorce". Since then, Slovakia has joined the European Union, the Schengen free travel zone, and NATO, and has adopted the euro as its currency.

Devin Castle

Built in the 9th century AD, Devin Castle is a short 20 minute bus ride away from Bratislava's city center. It overlooks the Danube river, which also serves as Slovakia's border with Austria. In the past, this castle marked the Kingdom of Hungary's Western limit. However, when the Cold War started around 1947, Devin Castle marked a significant boundary between the communist East and the capitalist West. Given how close Devin Castle was to neutral Austria, this area was heavily militarized to prevent Czechoslovakians from fleeing beyond the Iron Curtain and into the West. It was only after the Velvet Revolution of 1989 that this area was demilitarized, and that Slovakia's borders with Western Europe were opened.

Gate of Freedom

Just outside of Devin Castle, the Gate of Freedom commemorates the Iron Curtain that separared Slovakia from Western Europe. Erected in 2005, this monument is riddled with bullet holes, and is dedicated to the 400 or so people who died trying to flee from Czechoslovakia to the West. At the time, the country's Western borders were well fortified with high-voltage electric fences, and were heavily patrolled. This stands in striking contrast to how easily one can travel across the border today: since both Slovakia and Austria are part of the Schengen Zone, you can conveniently take a train from one to the other without even showing your passport. I found this monument to be a sobering reminder of how far Europe has come towards integration, and of the heavy restrictions many people in Eastern Europe faced during the Cold War.

Slavin Memorial

In contrast to the Gate of Freedom, the Slavin Memorial was erected in honor of the Soviet troops that liberated Bratislava from its Nazi occupiers in 1945, in the final stages of World War II. The USSR's Red Army kicked German troops out of the key Czechoslovakian cities of Bratislava and Brno, in a major offensive that resulted in the deaths of around 17,000 Soviet troops. On the top of the Slavin Memorial, one can make out a Soviet soldier squashing a swastika with his shoe. The monument is part of a military cemetary where over 6,000 Soviet troops lie, many of whom are in mass graves. Having grown up in France, I had been to many American cemetaries dedicated to the soldiers who died during D-Day. The Slavin Memorial resembled these in so many ways, and made me feel the same reverance I felt on the Normandy beaches. It felt like a hopeful reminder that, despite the countless political differences the USA has had with the USSR/Russia, our countries have at least occasionally fought on the same side, and could potentially do so again.
