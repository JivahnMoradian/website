HUNGARY

Budapest

Budapest (pronounced Budapesht) is the capital city of Hungary. It was founded in 1873 when three neighboring cities (Buda, Obua, and Pest) decided to unite. It is today one of the fastest growing cities in the European Union. Although Hungary still uses its own currency, the forint, many restaurants that I visited also accepted euros. I was delighted by how cheap food and most souvenirs were: a delicious and filling 2-person meal at an authentic Hungarian restaurant only cost my friend and me 10 euros! I was surprised to learn that Hungarian (or Magyar) is part of the Uralic language family, which means that it has more similarities with Finnish or Estonian than with the Slavic or Germanic languages spoken by its neighbors. I found Budapest to be a very vibrant city, with many pleasant hiking routes in the public parks and along the Danube river.

The Kingdom of Hungary was a powerful country that existed from 1000 until 1946. Its borders shifted substantially during this time, as it was subject to numerous skirmishes and invasions, such as those by the Mongols in 1241 and in 1285. In 1867, Hungary united with Austria to form the Austrio-Hungarian Empire ruled by the Habsburg royal family, and became one of the most powerful countries in Europe. Following its defeat in World War I, it was dissolved, and its territorial extent was significantly reduced. During World War II, the Kingdom of Hungary allied itself with Nazi Germany and the Axis Powers. It was invaded and occupied by the USSR in 1945, which marked the end of the monarchy. The Hungarian People's Republic was established in 1949, and was a satellite state of the Soviet Union.

The Hungarian Revolution of 1956 was a student-led revolution that brought moderate communist leader Imre Nagy to power. Nagy attempted to liberalize Hungary's communist party, which did not please Khrushchev, the leader of the USSR. When Nagy attempted to withdraw Hungary from the Warsaw Pact (the USSR and its allies' equivalent to NATO), the USSR invaded Hungary and placed the more ideologically acceptable Janos Kadar in charge. Nagy would be arrested and secretly executed in 1958. Hungary remained under Soviet influence until 1989, when the ruling communist party rebranded itself as the Hungarian Socialist Party, significantly modified the nation's constitution, and installed a multiparty democratic system of government.

Hungarian Parliament Building

This magnificent building was completed in 1902, and is the 2nd largest parliament building in the world (after the Palace of the Parliament in Bucharest). Building it required over 100,000 laborers. The Parliament building's Gothic architecture makes it an imposing and impressive structure. I found that the spaciousness of the square in front of it accentuated its size. Since its transition to democracy in 1989, Hungary's government has aligned itself more closely with the West, and has joined the European Union and NATO. That being said, it remains somewhat of a maverick in the European Union, as the country's right-wing leadership wants to maintain a fair amount of independence, and is at odds with some of the EU's liberal policies on LGBTQ rights and immigration.

Memorial for Victims of the German Occupation

This eerie statue is located next to Budapest's Liberty Square. Built in 2001, it depicts an eagle symbolizing Germany attacking the angel Gabriel. During World War II, Hungary allied itself with Nazi Germany and the Axis Powers hoping to reclaim the territories that it lost at the end of World War I, including parts of Czechoslovakia and Romania. Although many Hungarians, including the nation's regent Admiral Horthy, were wary of Germany, a large number were enthusiastic supporters of Hitler, especially the fascist Arrow Cross Party. In 1941, Hungary invaded the USSR alongside Germany, and was initially successful. However, by 1944 the tide of the war had turned against the Axis Powers. Despite being staunchly anticommunist, Horthy tried to covertly surrender to the USSR. To prevent this, Germany invaded and occupied Hungary, and even kidnapped Horthy's son to ensure his cooperation. The Arrow Cross Party then had a free reign to harrass and kill Hungarian Jews. The Memorial for Victims of the German Occupation was controversial because it portrays Hungary as a victim of Nazi oppression, when Hungary was in fact at least somewhat complicit. I was intrigued to see a placard next to the statue that accused Hungary's current right-wing government under Viktor Orban of historical revisionism. It is clear that Hungarians have significant differences in opinion when it comes to how they should remember their past.

Millenium Monument

The Millenium Monument is located in the center of Heroes' Square, and depicts many Hungarian historical figures. It was here that Imre Nagy was reburied in 1989, in a massive event that attracted over 200,000 people, and marked the end of communist single-party rule in Hungary. The square is conveniently located next to the Szechenyi thermal baths - Budapest is one of the only 3 capital cities in the world to have natural thermal springs (alongside Reykjavik in Iceland, and Sofia in Bulgaria). I would highly recommend checking these baths out as their temperatures of up to 40 degrees Celsius make them a highly relaxing activity. Heroes' Square is located at the end of Andrassy Avenue, a famous and busy street inspired by the Champs-Elysees in Paris, that is recognized as a World Heritage site. On this street is the House of Terror, a museum dedicated to commemorating the Hungarian victims to both Nazism and communism. Similar to the Monument to the Victims of the German Occupation, I got the sense that the museum sought to revise Hungary's history and to present the nation as a victim. Nevertheless, the testimonials of the Arrow Cross Party's victims, and the description of the restrictions that ordinary people faced during the communist regime, were extremely shocking. My experience in Budapest made me feel extremely grateful for the freedoms and the relative security that I take for granted.
