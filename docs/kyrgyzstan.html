Kyrgyzstan

I visited Kyrgyzstan in August 2023, as part of my Central Asia trip with my good friend from university Allen Liu. We took a 4-hour bus from Almaty (Kazakhstan) across the border to Bishkek, Kyrgyzstan’s capital city. Like most other countries in Central Asia, Kyrgyzstan is very safe, and we met several solo women travelers who confirmed that impression. Although getting into Kyrgyzstan was easy, getting out proved challenging. When we arrived at the Bishkek bus station to buy a ticket for Uzbekistan, we learned that all buses were filled up for the next 24 hours, so we had to delay our departure until the next day. The following day, we were running late for our bus, so we ordered a taxi using Yandex (a Russian app like Uber, but sooo much cheaper). Our driver was pulled over by the police, delaying us even further. Somehow, we caught our bus with under 5 minutes to spare.

Bishkek

Bishkek is a pleasant city to explore, but not one with a lot of touristic activities. We spent two days here, and I feel confident that one could easily get a good taste of the city within a day. Bishkek’s architecture feels Soviet in style, and it has a good number of parks and greenery, which made strolling around refreshing. I was surprised to notice that the city became much more vibrant at night, and many people were strolling around the park in the evening. Allen and I checked out the Osh Bazaar near the city center hoping to buy some souvenirs, but it turns out that almost no one at the market was selling touristic trinkets. Instead, they sold mundane items like food and clothing. What nerve these local merchants have to cater to local Kyrgyz residents instead of clueless American tourists!

  • Population (2024): 6.2 million
  • Life Expectancy (2024): 72.9 years
  • GDP Per Capita (PPP, 2023): $6,400
  • Currency: Kyrgyzstani Som
  • Main Language(s): Kyrgyz, Russian, Uzbek
  • Religion(s): 90% Islam, 7% Christian

The region that is now Kyrgyzstan has fallen under the rule of many different empires. It was controlled by the Mongols until the 18th century, then it was taken over by the Chinese Qing dynasty and by the Uzbek Kokand Khanate. By 1876, Kyrgyzstan fell under the control of the Russian Empire. After the Russian Revolution of 1917, Kyrgyzstan was incorporated into the Soviet Union as the Kirghiz Soviet Socialist Republic. Many Kyrgyz people resisted the Soviet push for collective agriculture and rapid industrialization because it conflicted with their traditional nomadic way of life. Although Kyrgyzstan is a very poor country with a GDP per capita of less than $2,000, it had better infrastructure than I would have expected, along with a decent life expectancy of 72 years and a literacy rate of almost 100%. So perhaps the Soviet influence had some benefits.

Kyrgyzstan is by far the most democratic country in Central Asia. Unlike its neighbors, whose history since independence has been dominated by one or two authoritarian leaders, Kyrgyzstan has had 6 presidents since 1991, including the region’s first female head of state. Nonetheless, Kyrgyzstan’s democracy is far from perfect. In 2005, many Kyrgyz people believed that Askar Akayev (the nation’s first president) was corrupt and becoming dictatorial. They overthrew him in what became known as the Tulip Revolution. Similar revolutions in 2010 and in 2020 also ousted Kurmanbek Bakiyev and Sooronbay Jeenbekov, Kyrgyzstan’s second and fifth presidents respectively. Although its democracy is far from perfect, I find it admirable that this small nation keeps such a vocal opposition. However, many people accuse the current president Sadyr Japarov of corruption, nepotism, and of democratic backsliding. Only time will tell where Kyrgyzstan’s political system will end up.

Ala Archa National Park

We went on a day trip to Ala Archa National Park in the Tian Shan mountains that are less than an hour’s drive away from Bishkek. We opted to hike up to the Racek Hut, in a 6-hour roundtrip journey. We passed by a stunning valley, and even a herd of wild horses. At the hut, we took a break and enjoyed the magnificent views of the peaks around us. The mountains in Kyrgyzstan are massive – Ala Archa Park contains several that are higher than any in Europe or in the contiguous United States, and Kyrgyzstan’s highest peak is a stunning 7,400m / 24,400ft high. We considered hiking further, but an incoming thunderstorm made us turn around without any regrets.

Ala-Too Square and Manas Statue

The Ala-Too Square marks the center of Bishkek. When Kyrgyzstan was part of the USSR, it was called Lenin Square and sported a large statue of Vladimir Lenin. After Kyrgyzstan gained its independence in 1991, they changed the square name and replaced the statue of Lenin with one of the Kyrgyz folk hero Manas. Poor Lenin was demoted to a less prominent location nearby. I think that swapping out a Soviet hero for a Kyrgyz one is a way for the country to reclaim its history and cultural heritage. In the back of the square, you can make out the Kyrgyz State Historical Museum, which boasts exhibits on traditional Kyrgyz customs and historical milestones.

As a Central Asian post-Soviet state, I expected Kyrgyzstan to have very few traces of Western influence. I was therefore shocked to see cinemas advertizing the Barbie movie. We could not pass up the opportunity to watch Barbie in Kyrgyzstan, so we went to the cinema - and even got to see it in English! This made me realize that American media culture really is widespread across the world.
